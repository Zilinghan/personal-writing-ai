6. Background:

   Federated learning (FL) is a machine learning method to train artificial intelligence (AI) models across multiple data holders without any data holder sharing its raw data. In FL, instead of moving data to a central location and training an AI model, each data holder trains a local AI model on its own data and only sends model updates to an orchestration server, which are then combined into a shared global model that benefits from everyone's knowledge. Think of it like a group of hospitals each separately studying the same disease and then sharing only their findings rather than their patient records. FL can play a critically important role in science and medicine, where institutions hold valuable datasets that cannot be shared due to patient privacy laws (like HIPAA), national security restrictions, or data governance agreements, but researchers still want to train the AI model using more data from multiple data holders.

   Despite federated learning's enormous potential, two major barriers prevented real-world scientific adoption. The first was infrastructure: existing FL tools were either too experimental for production use, too rigid to support the diverse computing environments at national laboratories, or too technically complex for domain scientists. The second was algorithmic efficiency: running FL across real institutions immediately exposes difficult engineering challenges — heterogeneous computing power causing fast sites to wait for slow ones, high communication costs from transmitting large model updates over the internet, commercial cloud costs spiraling without careful management, and the HPC scheduling problem where jobs on supercomputers wait in queues for hours before executing. This project was undertaken to simultaneously address both barriers: building a production-grade FL ecosystem and developing a suite of novel algorithms that make FL practical and efficient at scale.

7. Plain language summary:

   APPFL (Advanced Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning) was originally developed by researchers at Argonne before I joined, as described in a 2022 paper — it was a proof-of-concept framework that demonstrated FL was feasible, but it was experimental, difficult to deploy in practice, and supported only a narrow set of algorithms. When I joined the project in early 2023, I started by building on top of it: I developed APPFLx (appflx.link), a web platform that exposed FL as a service so that domain scientists at partner institutions could register their computing site and join a federated experiment without writing any custom infrastructure code, authenticating and transferring data through Globus, the secure data service standard across U.S. national laboratories. As I went on to develop FedCompass and a growing suite of other FL algorithms, it became clear that the original codebase was not ready to support real, diverse scientific deployments. I undertook a complete redesign of APPFL — rethinking the architecture from scratch to handle heterogeneous computing environments, broader security requirements, and a much wider range of FL scenarios including vertical, hierarchical, and decentralized settings — and published the resulting framework in a 2025 paper at CCGrid. I also built CADRE, a companion tool that verifies data at each participating site meets quality and format requirements before training begins, a practical step no prior framework had addressed.

   In parallel, I developed a series of novel algorithms that tackle efficiency problems that arise when FL runs across real, heterogeneous institutions rather than in a controlled lab setting. FedCompass addresses the straggler problem: in standard FL, all sites must finish each round before results are combined, so one slow participant holds everyone up. FedCompass estimates each site's computing speed on the fly and assigns it a personalized workload per round so that faster and slower participants finish at roughly the same time. It was published at ICLR 2024 and has 31 citations. FedSZ tackles communication cost: model updates can be enormous, and transmitting them over the internet every round is slow and expensive. FedSZ applies lossy compression to these updates before sending them, cutting their size dramatically while keeping the introduced error within a guaranteed mathematical bound. It was published at ICDCS 2024 and has 18 citations. FedCostAware addresses cloud spending, achieving over 70% cost reduction by scheduling resources with explicit awareness of monetary cost. FedQueue extends scheduling to DOE supercomputing facilities, where jobs sit in batch queues with unpredictable wait times before they can run, making cross-facility coordination particularly difficult. FedQueue was accepted at ICML 2026.

   I also applied FL to the problem of making large language models (LLMs) smaller and more deployable. LLMs like LLaMA are powerful but too large for many institutions to store or run on their own hardware. FedSpaLLM is a federated pruning method: each institution contributes to identifying which model parameters can be safely removed, without sharing its private data, and the result is a leaner model that was compressed using knowledge distributed across multiple sites. It was published at NAACL 2025 and has 19 citations. A related case study on federated fine-tuning of LLaMA 2 across cloud and HPC resources, published in Computing in Science & Engineering (CiSE 2024), demonstrated that these FL workflows can run end-to-end on real DOE computing infrastructure.

6. Background:

   Two major unmet needs in medicine motivated this project. First, many patients with rare genetic diseases — particularly rare heritable disorders of the aorta (the body's main artery), called genetic aortopathies — go undiagnosed for years or decades. These conditions can cause the aorta to rupture without warning, which is often fatal. The diseases are rare enough that most physicians have never seen a case, so patients' symptoms are routinely attributed to other causes. If AI could automatically screen clinical records and flag at-risk patients, it could save lives by getting patients to specialists before a catastrophic event occurs.

   Second, pathology — the analysis of tissue samples under a microscope to diagnose cancer and other diseases — is one of the most demanding and time-consuming tasks in medicine. Each tissue slide is a gigapixel-scale image (roughly the size of a billboard reduced to a microscope slide), and pathologists must manually examine every region. AI models that can assist with or automate this analysis have enormous potential to make cancer diagnosis faster, more consistent, and more accessible globally — but building such models requires enormous amounts of specialized training data and novel AI architectures capable of processing images of this scale.

7. Plain language summary:

   Goal 1 — LLMs for Rare Disease Identification: I co-led the development of a pipeline that uses open-source large language models (AI systems trained to understand text, similar to ChatGPT but using freely available models) to screen electronic health records and identify patients who may have undiagnosed rare genetic aortopathies. The pipeline reads physicians' free-text clinical notes, extracts relevant symptoms and findings, and produces a risk score for each patient. Using open-source models rather than proprietary ones was a deliberate choice: it means the system can run entirely within a hospital's own IT infrastructure without patient data ever leaving the institution — preserving privacy while achieving high sensitivity for catching at-risk patients. I was a co-first author on this work (equal contribution with two colleagues), led the LLM pipeline development and evaluation, and presented the results at the AMIA 2025 Informatics Summit, the leading biomedical informatics conference.

   Goal 2 — Foundation Models for Digital Histopathology: I contributed to a series of projects developing and evaluating AI models for analyzing pathology images. A key challenge is that a single whole-slide pathology image contains billions of pixels — far too large for any AI model to process at once. The field has developed "tile-based" approaches that divide the slide into thousands of small image patches, analyze each patch independently, and then combine the results. My contributions included work on TICON (a model that better captures the spatial relationships between patches), PixCell (a generative AI model that can produce realistic synthetic pathology images, which can be used to augment training data), and Gen-SIS (a method that uses generative AI to improve self-supervised learning). I also contributed to developing efficient image compression methods for pathology images that reduce storage and transmission costs — directly relevant to running federated learning across medical institutions.

   Goal 3 — Polygenic Risk and Multi-Ancestry Genomics: I contributed to a large-scale genetic analysis study that examined how genetic variants across the human genome interact to influence risk across more than 1,000 different traits, using data from biobanks spanning multiple ancestries worldwide. This type of analysis — called a pleiotropy study — helps scientists understand which genetic variants affect multiple traits simultaneously, which has implications for understanding disease mechanisms and identifying potential drug targets. This work is currently under review at Nature.

6. Background:

   As AI systems — especially large language models (LLMs) — have become increasingly powerful, the scientific community has begun to ask whether they can meaningfully assist researchers in doing science itself: writing code, analyzing data, reviewing literature, solving domain-specific problems in physics, chemistry, or biology. These are very different from the general tasks (writing emails, summarizing documents) that most AI benchmarks test for. To use AI effectively in science, the community needs two things: good benchmarks that honestly test whether AI models can do real scientific work, and real applications that demonstrate AI's value in scientific workflows.

   A parallel need exists in AI for astrophysics: when gravitational waves are detected by LIGO and Virgo observatories, astronomers must quickly coordinate telescope observations worldwide to capture the electromagnetic counterpart to the event (called a radio afterglow) before it fades. This follow-up process currently requires significant manual coordination. An automated, AI-driven system that can analyze radio telescope data and coordinate follow-up in real time would accelerate the scientific discovery pipeline. Finally, the AuroraGPT project aims to develop large language models specifically trained on scientific text and data, using Argonne's Aurora exascale supercomputer — one of the world's fastest computers, capable of more than one quintillion calculations per second — to train models at a scale beyond what most institutions can achieve.

7. Plain language summary:

   Goal 1 — SciCode (Benchmark for Scientific Coding): I am a major contributor to SciCode, an open-source benchmark designed to test whether AI models can generate code that solves realistic scientific research problems. Existing coding benchmarks primarily test software engineering skills (like implementing standard algorithms), but they do not capture what scientific coding actually looks like — which involves implementing numerical methods, simulations, and data analysis pipelines drawn from physics, mathematics, chemistry, and biology. SciCode was developed in collaboration with domain scientists across multiple disciplines who contributed problems from their own research. The benchmark has been widely adopted by the AI community as a rigorous test of scientific AI capabilities, with 196 GitHub stars and 34 forks as of May 2026.

   Goal 2 — EAIRA (Evaluating AI as Scientific Research Assistants): SciCode focuses on code generation; EAIRA takes a broader view, establishing a comprehensive methodology for evaluating how well any AI model can function as a scientific research assistant across a wide range of tasks. This includes not just coding but also scientific reasoning, literature synthesis, experimental design advice, and domain-specific problem solving across multiple scientific fields. The work fills an important gap: before EAIRA, there was no standard, rigorous way to compare AI models on their usefulness for science. The methodology paper has already accumulated 11 citations as a preprint, indicating strong community interest.

   Goal 3 — RADAR (AI for Gravitational-Wave Follow-Up): I contributed to RADAR, a federated AI framework for the rapid follow-up of gravitational-wave events with radio telescopes. When gravitational waves are detected — ripples in spacetime caused by cataclysmic events like colliding neutron stars — a global network of radio telescopes needs to quickly observe the same region of sky to detect the electromagnetic signal. RADAR automates this process: it uses federated learning to train AI models on radio telescope data distributed across observatories, then deploys those models to analyze incoming data and trigger coordinated follow-up observations in near real-time. RADAR was published in The Astrophysical Journal Supplement Series, one of the leading journals in astrophysics.
