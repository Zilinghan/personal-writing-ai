6. Background:

   Federated learning (FL) is a machine learning method to train artificial intelligence (AI) models across multiple data holders without any data holder sharing its raw data. In FL, instead of moving data to a central location and training an AI model, each data holder trains a local AI model on its own data and only sends model updates to an orchestration server, which are then combined into a shared global model that benefits from everyone's knowledge. Think of it like a group of hospitals each separately studying the same disease and then sharing only their findings rather than their patient records. FL can play a critically important role in science and medicine, where institutions hold valuable datasets that cannot be shared due to patient privacy laws (like HIPAA), national security restrictions, or data governance agreements, but researchers still want to train the AI model using more data from multiple data holders.

   Despite federated learning's enormous potential, two major barriers prevented real-world scientific adoption. The first was infrastructure: existing FL tools were either too experimental for production use, too rigid to support the diverse computing environments at national laboratories, or too technically complex for domain scientists. The second was algorithmic efficiency: running FL across real institutions immediately exposes difficult engineering challenges — heterogeneous computing power causing fast sites to wait for slow ones, high communication costs from transmitting large model updates over the internet, commercial cloud costs spiraling without careful management, and the HPC scheduling problem where jobs on supercomputers wait in queues for hours before executing. This project was undertaken to simultaneously address both barriers: building a production-grade FL ecosystem and developing a suite of novel algorithms that make FL practical and efficient at scale.

7. Plain language summary:

    Researchers at Argonne National Laboratory developed the original APPFL (Advanced Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning) framework in 2022 before I joined Argonne. At that time, APPFL was a proof-of-concept framework for demonstrating the feasibility of FL, but it was experimental and only supported a narrow set of algorithms. The barrier for most domain scientists, who do not have sufficient computer science background, to use APPFL for federated learning experiments was very high back then. When I joined the project in early 2023 as a graduate research aide, I started by building on top of it: I developed APPFLx (https://appflx.link/), a web platform that provided FL as a service so that domain scientists at partner institutions could easily register their computing site through a simple interface and join an FL experiment without writing any custom infrastructure code. 

    As teams began using APPFLx for real experiments, I observed firsthand the inefficiencies of existing FL algorithms when FL runs across real and heterogeneous institutions rather than in a controlled lab setting. This motivated me to develop a series of novel FL algorithms targeting specific bottlenecks. I led the development of FedCompass, which addresses the straggler problem: in standard FL, all participants must wait for the slowest site to finish each round, which might significantly slow down the training process. FedCompass estimates each site’s computing speed on the fly and assigns a personalized workload so that faster and slower participants finish at roughly the same time. It was published at ICLR 2024 and has 32 citations. I also contributed to FedSZ, which reduces communication cost by applying mathematically bounded lossy compression to model updates before transmission, published at ICDCS 2024 with 20 citations, and to FedSpaLLM, which makes large language models smaller and more deployable by collaboratively identifying removable parameters across institutions without sharing private data, published at NAACL 2025 with 19 citations. I supervised the development of FedCostAware, which reduces cloud computing costs by over 70% through cost-aware scheduling, and FedQueue, which handles the challenge of unpredictable HPC batch queue wait times in cross-facility training, accepted at ICML 2026.

    As the algorithm suite expanded and the user community grew, the original APPFL codebase became a limiting factor for usability and scalability. Therefore, I undertook a complete redesign of the framework - rethinking the architecture from scratch to handle heterogeneous computing environments, broader security requirements, and a wider range of FL scenarios - and published the result in a 2025 paper at CCGrid. The redesigned, production-level framework attracted significant adoption: it was taken up by the NIH Bridge2AI flagship projects to train privacy-preserving biomedical AI models, applied to federated learning for ophthalmology (AI-READI initiative - https://aireadi.org/) and neurological voice biomarkers (https://b2ai-voice.org/). It is also deployed across multiple DOE national laboratories to build federation networks among leadership-class supercomputing facilities. This track record of real-world impact led to new federal investment, including an ASCR award titled “Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning for Science: Building Sustainable and Trustworthy Foundation Models” (https://ai4s-ppfl.github.io/) [and maybe the TRAC].

6. Background:

    With the rapid development of AI techniques, particularly large foundation models, dramatic growth in available computing power, and the increasing availability of large-scale biomedical datasets, we found enormous potential to develop biomedical AI models that can automate complex medical workflows to improve diagnostic efficiency and accuracy and ultimately improve patient outcomes. 

    Within this broader opportunity, two specific unmet needs motivated this project. First, pathology, the analysis of tissue samples under a microscope to diagnose cancer and other diseases, is one of the most demanding and time-consuming tasks in medicine, and one where AI has enormous but largely unrealize potential. Each whole-slide pathology image (WSI) is a gigapixel-scale file, and pathologists must manually examine every region. Also, due to the large size of WSI, any existing AI models cannot process it at once. Standard practice divides each slide into thousands of small tiles that are processed independently, but this approach discards the spatial relationships between tissue regions that pathologists rely on - neighboring structures often carry complementary diagnostic information that is lost when tiles are analyzed in isolation. Training powerful AI models for pathology faces further compounding challenges: 
    •	preparing annotated pathology images for AI model training requires expert pathologists and is extremely expensive and time-consuming;
    •	strict patient privacy rules prevent institutions from freely sharing slide data to build larger training sets;
    •	standard data augmentation methods (methods to generate more training images) from general computer vision transfer poorly to histology, where tissue texture and cell morphology carry precise biological meaning that generic transformations distort;
    •	there is also a practical infrastructure problem: a large pathology center produces over one million digital slides per year, generating petabytes of data whose long-term storage can cost over $100,000 annually, making efficient compression, which does not sacrifice the fine-grained detail on which diagnoses depend, an important open problem as well.
    Therefore, in the project, we aim to address the following challenges in pathology AI: contextual slide-level understanding, data-efficient and privacy-preserving model training, domain-appropriate pathology image augmentation and generative data synthesis, and scalable WSI storage.

    Second, we focused on rare genetic diseases, particularly rare heritable disorders of the aorta (the body’s main artery), called genetic aortopathies. Many patients with rare genetic aortopathies are frequently undiagnosed for years or decades: their clinical presentations are highly heterogeneous, with symptoms that overlap with many common conditions, and most primary care physicians have never encountered a case, making early recognition through standard clinical practice nearly impossible. Current screening relies on specialist referral and manual chart review, which are far too slow and labor-intensive to systematically reach the large patient populations at risk. The key insight motivating our work is that electronic health records (EHRs) already contain the signals needed to flag at-risk patients. Physicians’ free-text clinical notes record observations about connective tissue features, cardiovascular findings, and family history that collectively point toward a genetic aeropathy, but no automated tool existed to extract and act on these signals at scale. Large language models (LLMs), with their ability to read, interpret, and reason over unstructured clinical text, offer a promising way to automate this screening by parsing physician notes at scale, identifying relevant clinical features, and flagging patients warranting genetic testing. However, a further constraint is that EHR data is protected health information and cannot be sent to external cloud services, ruling out proprietary LLM APIs. This created a specific technical problem: building an open-source LLM pipeline capable of reading and reasoning over unstructured clinical notes, running entirely within a hospital’s own infrastructure, and producing referrals for genetic testing.


7. Plain language summary:

   To address the rare aortopathy screening problem, I co-led the development of an open-source LLM pipeline that reads physicians' free-text clinical notes in electronic health records, extracts relevant clinical signals — connective tissue features, cardiovascular findings, family history — and produces ranked referrals for genetic testing. A key design decision was to build the pipeline entirely around open-source models such as LLaMA and Mistral, which can be deployed and run within a hospital's own IT infrastructure so that protected patient data never leaves the institution. This directly addresses the privacy barrier that rules out commercial cloud APIs. I was a co-first author on this work (equal contribution), led the model selection, pipeline implementation, and clinical validation experiments, and presented the results at the AMIA 2025 Informatics Summit, the leading biomedical informatics conference.    For pathology AI, I contributed to a series of projects that each targeted one of the specific challenges described above. To address the loss of spatial context in tile-based pipelines, I contributed to TICON, a slide-level tile contextualizer that learns to enrich individual tile embeddings with information from neighboring regions through a masked modeling pretraining strategy, improving performance on both tile-level and slide-level diagnostic tasks. To address data scarcity, privacy constraints on data sharing, and the need for domain-appropriate augmentation, I contributed to PixCell, the first generative foundation model for histopathology, trained on over 30 million patches from 69,000 slides; PixCell can generate realistic synthetic pathology images that institutions can share in place of real patient data, and its synthetic outputs can augment training sets without requiring raw data transfer. 6. Background:

   As AI systems — especially large language models (LLMs) — have become increasingly powerful, the scientific community has begun to ask whether they can meaningfully assist researchers in doing science itself: writing code, analyzing data, reviewing literature, solving domain-specific problems in physics, chemistry, or biology. These are very different from the general tasks (writing emails, summarizing documents) that most AI benchmarks test for. To use AI effectively in science, the community needs two things: good benchmarks that honestly test whether AI models can do real scientific work, and real applications that demonstrate AI's value in scientific workflows.

   A parallel need exists in AI for astrophysics: when gravitational waves are detected by LIGO and Virgo observatories, astronomers must quickly coordinate telescope observations worldwide to capture the electromagnetic counterpart to the event (called a radio afterglow) before it fades. This follow-up process currently requires significant manual coordination. An automated, AI-driven system that can analyze radio telescope data and coordinate follow-up in real time would accelerate the scientific discovery pipeline. Finally, the AuroraGPT project aims to develop large language models specifically trained on scientific text and data, using Argonne's Aurora exascale supercomputer — one of the world's fastest computers, capable of more than one quintillion calculations per second — to train models at a scale beyond what most institutions can achieve.

7. Plain language summary:

   Goal 1 — SciCode (Benchmark for Scientific Coding): I am a major contributor to SciCode, an open-source benchmark designed to test whether AI models can generate code that solves realistic scientific research problems. Existing coding benchmarks primarily test software engineering skills (like implementing standard algorithms), but they do not capture what scientific coding actually looks like — which involves implementing numerical methods, simulations, and data analysis pipelines drawn from physics, mathematics, chemistry, and biology. SciCode was developed in collaboration with domain scientists across multiple disciplines who contributed problems from their own research. The benchmark has been widely adopted by the AI community as a rigorous test of scientific AI capabilities, with 196 GitHub stars and 34 forks as of May 2026.

   Goal 2 — EAIRA (Evaluating AI as Scientific Research Assistants): SciCode focuses on code generation; EAIRA takes a broader view, establishing a comprehensive methodology for evaluating how well any AI model can function as a scientific research assistant across a wide range of tasks. This includes not just coding but also scientific reasoning, literature synthesis, experimental design advice, and domain-specific problem solving across multiple scientific fields. The work fills an important gap: before EAIRA, there was no standard, rigorous way to compare AI models on their usefulness for science. The methodology paper has already accumulated 11 citations as a preprint, indicating strong community interest.

   Goal 3 — RADAR (AI for Gravitational-Wave Follow-Up): I contributed to RADAR, a federated AI framework for the rapid follow-up of gravitational-wave events with radio telescopes.
