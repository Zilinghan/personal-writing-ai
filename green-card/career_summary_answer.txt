6. Background:

   Federated learning (FL) is a distributed machine learning paradigm that
   enables multiple institutions to collaboratively train AI models without
   sharing their underlying raw data. Each participating institution trains a
   local model on its private dataset and shares only model parameters (weights
   or gradients), which are aggregated into a shared global model. This approach
   is critically important in scientific and biomedical research, where valuable
   datasets exist across many institutions but cannot be pooled due to privacy
   regulations (such as HIPAA for patient health data), data governance
   agreements, or national security considerations at government laboratories.

   Despite federated learning's enormous promise for enabling collaborative
   science, adoption was severely limited by the lack of robust, production-ready
   software infrastructure. Existing FL tools were either too experimental for
   real deployments, too rigid to support the heterogeneous computing environments
   at DOE national laboratories (spanning on-premises HPC clusters, edge devices,
   and commercial cloud), or too complex for domain scientists who are not
   professional software engineers. The APPFL project was undertaken to solve
   these fundamental problems and build a comprehensive, flexible, open-source FL
   ecosystem that would make privacy-preserving collaborative AI accessible
   across the national laboratory network and the broader scientific community.

7. Plain language summary:

   APPFL (Advanced Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning) is an open-source
   Python software framework hosted at github.com/APPFL/APPFL that provides
   all the essential building blocks for deploying federated learning in real
   scientific applications. The framework is architected around a plugin-based,
   modular design where each functional component — FL aggregation algorithms,
   communication backends (supporting gRPC, MPI, and Globus Compute), privacy
   protection mechanisms (differential privacy and secure aggregation), data
   compression modules, and client/server configurations — can be independently
   configured or swapped. This modularity allows researchers to prototype novel
   FL algorithms with minimal code changes, while practitioners can deploy
   production-grade FL workflows using standard, battle-tested configurations.
   The framework supports the full FL lifecycle: dataset management, training
   coordination, model aggregation, evaluation, and logging.

   Alongside the core framework, the project produced APPFLx (appflx.link), a
   web-based platform delivering federated learning as a service. Rather than
   requiring each institution to set up and maintain their own FL infrastructure,
   APPFLx allows domain scientists to register their computing sites through a
   web interface, specify their local datasets, and participate in federated
   training experiments without writing custom distributed systems code. The
   platform integrates with Globus for secure authentication and data transfer —
   a widely used infrastructure across DOE national laboratories — making it
   particularly well-suited to the DOE ecosystem. APPFLx has been used in
   biomedical research to enable hospitals and health systems to train AI models
   collaboratively without ever moving patient data outside their institutional
   firewalls.

   Throughout the project, several technically significant capabilities were
   developed and integrated into the APPFL ecosystem. A computing-power-aware
   scheduling algorithm (FedCompass, published at ICLR 2024) was created to
   handle the fundamental challenge of heterogeneous client devices in cross-silo
   FL, where different institutions have vastly different computational resources
   and slower clients can bottleneck the entire training process. Cost-aware
   scheduling (FedCostAware) was developed to minimize monetary costs when
   running FL jobs on commercial cloud infrastructure, reducing costs by over 70%.
   Cross-facility FL capabilities were built to run training experiments spanning
   multiple DOE supercomputers simultaneously. Data readiness tooling (CADRE) was
   added to verify data quality and format consistency across federated sites
   before training begins, addressing a key practical barrier to real deployments.

   The project also contributed to integrating the framework with broader DOE
   computing infrastructure, including Globus Compute for remote function
   execution and the FABRIC testbed for network-layer experiments. This sustained,
   multi-year engineering and research effort transformed APPFL from an
   experimental prototype into a mature ecosystem adopted by multiple national
   laboratories.

6. Background:

   One of the most persistent practical challenges in federated learning is
   the "straggler problem": in any cross-silo FL deployment, participating
   institutions have vastly different computational resources, network speeds,
   and workloads. When a synchronous FL protocol is used, the entire training
   round cannot proceed until the slowest client finishes — meaning that a
   single institution with outdated hardware or heavy existing load can
   dramatically slow the entire federated training process for all participants.
   This significantly limits the practical scalability of FL across real-world
   scientific institutions.

   Existing solutions to this problem fell into two categories: purely
   synchronous approaches (which suffer from the straggler problem) and purely
   asynchronous approaches (which tend to produce less accurate models due to
   gradient staleness and inconsistent model updates). Neither adequately solved
   the problem for the heterogeneous computing environments found across DOE
   national laboratories and academic research sites, where computational
   capacity can differ by orders of magnitude between participants. This project
   aimed to design a fundamentally better scheduling strategy that could achieve
   training efficiency close to asynchronous methods while maintaining the model
   quality of synchronous approaches.

7. Plain language summary:

   FedCompass (Federated learning with a Computing Power Aware Scheduler) is a
   novel FL training algorithm that addresses the heterogeneous client problem
   by dynamically estimating each client's computing capacity and strategically
   scheduling how many model updates each client should submit per training
   round. Instead of waiting for all clients to finish (synchronous) or
   collecting updates as they arrive without coordination (asynchronous),
   FedCompass assigns each client a personalized number of "steps" to compute
   before sending updates. Faster clients are assigned more work, and slower
   clients are assigned less, so that all clients tend to finish their
   assignments at approximately the same time. This approach dramatically
   reduces idle waiting time at the server while keeping model updates fresh
   and consistent.

   The algorithm uses a lightweight online estimation procedure to infer each
   client's computing speed from their observed response times, without requiring
   clients to self-report their capacities. The scheduling decisions are computed
   centrally at the server before each round, adding negligible overhead. The
   result is a training protocol that achieves near-synchronous model quality
   with significantly higher throughput on heterogeneous infrastructure.

   A companion paper on this project (FedSZ, ICDCS 2024) addressed the related
   problem of communication overhead in FL, which is a bottleneck for clients
   with limited network bandwidth. FedSZ integrates error-bounded lossy
   compression techniques (from Argonne's SZ compression library) into the FL
   communication protocol, substantially reducing the volume of data transmitted
   without meaningfully degrading model accuracy. Together, FedCompass and FedSZ
   represent a comprehensive solution to the twin bottlenecks of compute and
   communication heterogeneity in real-world FL deployments.

   More recently, the scheduling ideas from FedCompass were extended to the
   multi-facility HPC setting in FedQueue (ICML 2026), which addresses the
   additional complexity of batch job queue systems on supercomputers, where
   training jobs must be submitted to a job scheduler and may wait in a queue
   before execution. FedQueue accounts for queue wait times in the scheduling
   strategy, enabling efficient FL training across multiple supercomputing
   facilities simultaneously.

6. Background:

   Two major unmet needs in medicine and biological research motivated this
   project. First, many patients with rare genetic diseases — particularly rare
   connective tissue disorders and genetic aortopathies — go undiagnosed for
   years or even decades because the diseases are uncommon, their presentations
   are variable, and most clinicians lack specialized expertise to recognize them.
   Automated AI tools that can screen electronic health records (EHR) and flag
   patients at risk could dramatically accelerate diagnosis. Second, digital
   pathology — the computerized analysis of tissue biopsy slides — has enormous
   potential to make cancer diagnosis faster, more reproducible, and more
   accessible, but existing AI models are limited by the lack of large-scale
   annotated training datasets and by computational constraints when processing
   gigapixel whole-slide images.

   This project aimed to develop and apply state-of-the-art AI methods to both
   problems, leveraging large language models (LLMs) for clinical text analysis
   and deep learning vision models for histopathology image analysis.

7. Plain language summary:

   In the rare disease work, the project applied open-source large language
   models (such as LLaMA and Mistral) to retrospectively screen clinical notes
   in electronic health records to identify patients with undiagnosed rare genetic
   aortopathies — heritable disorders that cause the aorta (the body's largest
   artery) to abnormally dilate or dissect, with potentially fatal consequences
   if undetected. The LLM pipeline was designed to extract relevant clinical
   features from unstructured free-text notes, classify patient risk, and
   prioritize candidates for genetic testing and specialist referral. This work
   represents one of the first demonstrations of using open-source LLMs (rather
   than proprietary models) for rare disease identification in clinical text,
   preserving patient privacy while achieving high diagnostic sensitivity.

   In the digital histopathology work, the project contributed to developing
   and evaluating novel deep learning architectures for analyzing whole-slide
   pathology images. These images, which can be gigapixels in size, present
   unique challenges: no single AI model can process the entire image at once,
   so the field relies on "multiple instance learning" approaches that aggregate
   features from thousands of small image tiles. Contributions included TICON
   (a slide-level model that contextualizes tile features spatially), PixCell
   (a generative foundation model for histopathology images that can synthesize
   realistic pathology images and support downstream analysis), and Gen-SIS
   (a self-supervised learning approach using generative models for data
   augmentation). The project also developed efficient image compression methods
   tailored to pathology images to reduce storage and transmission costs in
   federated pathology workflows.

   A third component of the biomedical work involved contributing to large-scale
   polygenic risk studies — analyses that combine genetic variants across the
   genome to predict disease risk — including a genome-wide pleiotropy study
   spanning over 1,000 traits across global biobanks, which is currently under
   review at Nature.

6. Background:

   Large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 have demonstrated remarkable
   capabilities across general language tasks, but their utility for specialized
   scientific research remains less well understood. Scientists need AI models
   that can reason about complex physical systems, assist with mathematical
   derivations, generate and debug scientific code, and interface with domain-
   specific knowledge in fields like physics, chemistry, and biology. The
   AuroraGPT project at Argonne National Laboratory is an effort to develop and
   evaluate LLMs specifically trained and fine-tuned for scientific applications,
   leveraging the Aurora exascale supercomputer — one of the world's most powerful
   computing systems with over one exaflop of computational capacity.

   A related challenge is evaluating whether existing LLMs are truly capable
   scientific assistants. Most LLM benchmarks test general knowledge and
   reasoning, but few evaluate models on realistic scientific research tasks
   such as writing functional scientific code, interpreting experimental results,
   or performing domain-specific reasoning. The EAIRA project addressed this
   gap by developing a rigorous evaluation methodology for assessing AI models
   as scientific research assistants.

7. Plain language summary:

   The AuroraGPT work involved contributing to the post-pretraining and evaluation
   of large language models on DOE supercomputing resources. Post-pretraining
   encompasses techniques like instruction fine-tuning, reinforcement learning
   from human feedback (RLHF), and domain-specific continued pretraining, which
   adapt a base LLM to be more helpful, accurate, and aligned with scientific
   use cases. Working on a system like Aurora provides access to computational
   resources that are orders of magnitude larger than commercial cloud instances,
   enabling experiments with model scales and dataset sizes not otherwise feasible.

   The EAIRA project (Establishing a Methodology for Evaluating AI Models as
   Scientific Research Assistants) developed a comprehensive framework for
   evaluating how well LLMs perform on realistic scientific tasks. The evaluation
   covers multiple scientific domains and task types, assessing models on their
   ability to assist with scientific coding, data analysis, literature synthesis,
   and domain-specific problem solving. This methodology fills an important gap
   in the AI evaluation literature by moving beyond toy benchmarks to tasks
   that reflect what scientists actually need from AI assistants.

   The FedSpaLLM project (NAACL 2025) addressed the orthogonal challenge of
   making LLMs smaller and more efficient through federated pruning — a technique
   that uses federated learning to collaboratively identify and remove redundant
   parameters from a large language model without centralizing the training data.
   This approach enables privacy-preserving model compression across institutions
   that hold private data but want to benefit from a shared, compact LLM.
